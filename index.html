Session 2 · Part A

Descriptive Statistics

The tools for summarising what your data actually looks like — before drawing any conclusions.

Descriptive Statistics

Two Ways to Describe Data

Once you have collected your data, you have two main approaches to describe it:

🔢 Numerical Method

  • More precise and objective
  • Compute averages (Mean, Median, Mode)
  • Compute variation (SD, Variance, Range)
  • Gives you exact numbers to compare

📊 Graphical Method

  • Visualises the shape of the data
  • Frequency tables, bar charts, line graphs
  • Box plots, histograms
  • Helps identify patterns and outliers quickly
💡 In practice you use both together. Start with a graph to "see" the data, then use numbers to be precise about what you saw.
Numerical Method

Measures of Central Tendency

These are the three types of "average" — they all try to describe the centre of your data, but they do it differently.

➕

What it is

Add all values together, then divide by how many values there are. The classic "average".

✅

Best used when

Data is on an interval or ratio scale with no extreme outliers.

⚠️

Limitation

One extreme value (outlier) can pull the mean up or down, giving a distorted picture.

💡 Example

5 employees earn: GHS 2,000 · 2,500 · 2,800 · 3,000 · 20,000
Mean = (2000+2500+2800+3000+20000) ÷ 5 = GHS 6,060 — but 4 out of 5 people earn far less! The one high salary distorted the mean. Here, median would be better.

Formula
x̄ = (x₁ + x₂ + ... + xₙ) / n
Where x̄ = mean, x = each value, n = total number of values

The middle value when all scores are sorted in order. Half the data falls above it, half below. Great when you have extreme values.

💡 Example

Scores in order: 40, 55, 62, 68, 72, 85, 90 → Median = 68 (the 4th of 7 values)

Even number of values: 40, 55, 62, 68, 72, 85 → Median = (62 + 68) ÷ 2 = 65

📌 Going back to the salary example: 2000, 2500, 2800, 3000, 20000 → Median = GHS 2,800. This is far more representative of what most employees actually earn.

The value that appears most frequently in your data. The only average that works with nominal (category) data.

💡 Example

Survey question: "Which phone brand do you use?" Responses: Samsung, iPhone, Samsung, Tecno, Samsung, iPhone, Tecno, Samsung → Mode = Samsung (appears 4 times). You can't calculate a mean or median from brand names — only the mode works.

⚡ A bimodal distribution has two modes (two values appear equally often). A distribution with no repeating values has no mode.
Descriptive Statistics

Which Average Should You Use?

The correct choice depends on your scale of measurement and whether outliers are present.

MeasureScale RequiredUse When…Avoid When…
Mean Interval or Ratio Data is roughly symmetric; no extreme outliers Data is skewed or has outliers — it will be misleading
Median Ordinal, Interval, or Ratio Data has extreme values or is ranked (e.g. income, house prices, Likert scales) When you need to use the result in further calculations
Mode Nominal (categorical) Data is in categories — the "most common" answer matters (e.g. most popular product) Continuous data — the mode may not be meaningful
💡 Business Decision Tip

A clothing retailer wants to know the most popular shoe size to stock. Mode is the right answer — not mean (size 9.3?) or median. But to report average monthly revenue, use the Mean (if no extreme months) or Median (if one bumper month skews things).

Numerical Method

Measures of Variation (Dispersion)

Two datasets can have the same average but look completely different. Variation tells you how spread out your data is from the centre.

💡 Why It Matters

Team A scores: 50, 50, 50, 50 → Mean = 50, no variation
Team B scores: 10, 30, 70, 90 → Mean = 50, huge variation
Same average, completely different performance. Variation reveals the full story.

MeasureFormula / IdeaBest ForWeakness
Range Max − Min Quick overview of the spread Heavily affected by extreme values; ignores all middle data
Interquartile Range (IQR) Q3 − Q1 (middle 50% of data) When outliers are present; used in box plots Still ignores the top 25% and bottom 25%
Variance Average of squared deviations from the mean Foundation for standard deviation; used in many tests Units are squared (e.g. GHS²) — hard to interpret directly
Standard Deviation (SD) √Variance — back in original units Most common; tells you the "typical" distance from the mean Still affected by outliers (less than range, but still)
Coefficient of Variation (CV) (SD / Mean) × 100% Comparing variation between datasets with different means/units Meaningless when the mean is zero or near zero
Formulas

Population vs Sample Formulas

There are two versions of variance and standard deviation — one for when you have the entire population, one for when you only have a sample. In most business research, you'll use the sample versions.

🌍 Population (σ)

  • You have data on everyone in the group
  • Variance: σ² = Σ(x − μ)² / N
  • SD: σ = √σ²
  • CV: (σ / μ) × 100%
  • Denominator: N (total population)

👥 Sample (s)

  • You have data on a subset of the group
  • Variance: s² = Σ(x − x̄)² / (n−1)
  • SD: s = √s²
  • CV: (s / x̄) × 100%
  • Denominator: n−1 (Bessel's correction)
❓ Why n−1 for samples? Using n would underestimate the true spread. Subtracting 1 corrects for the fact that your sample doesn't capture all the variation in the population. This is called Bessel's correction.
💡 Worked Example (Sample SD)

Monthly profits (GHS 000s): 10, 14, 12, 18, 16 → Mean (x̄) = 14
Deviations²: (10−14)²=16, (14−14)²=0, (12−14)²=4, (18−14)²=16, (16−14)²=4
Variance s² = (16+0+4+16+4) / (5−1) = 40/4 = 10
Standard Deviation s = √10 ≈ GHS 3,162 — typical profit varies by about GHS 3,162 from the mean.

Session 2 · Part B

Hypothesis Testing

How to formally test whether your research claim is supported by the data.

Hypothesis Testing

What is a Hypothesis?

A hypothesis is a testable statement that explains a relationship or difference. The key word is testable — you must be able to collect data and check whether the statement holds.

❌ NOT a Good Hypothesis

  • "Empowerment programmes may enhance performance" (too vague — how will you test "may"?)
  • "People tend to prefer quality" (no specific measurable prediction)
  • "Companies might benefit from training"

✅ A Good Hypothesis

  • States 2+ measurable variables
  • States an IV and a DV
  • States a clear relationship or difference
  • "Good internal control significantly enhances corporate performance"
TypeSymbolSays…Example
Null Hypothesis H₀ "Nothing is happening" — no difference, no relationship H₀: There is no difference in performance between trained and untrained staff
Alternative Hypothesis H₁ "Something is happening" — there IS a difference or relationship H₁: Trained staff significantly outperform untrained staff
🔑 You never prove H₁ directly. Instead, you try to disprove H₀. If the data strongly contradicts H₀, you reject H₀ and accept H₁.
Hypothesis Testing

5 Steps in Hypothesis Testing

StepWhat You DoPlain English
1 State H₀ and H₁ Write your "nothing is happening" statement (H₀) and your "something is happening" prediction (H₁)
2 Choose test & significance level Pick the right statistical test (t-test, ANOVA, etc.) and set your threshold — usually α = 0.05 (5%)
3 State the decision rule Define when you'll reject H₀ — e.g., "I'll reject H₀ if p-value < 0.05"
4 Compute test statistic & p-value Run the calculation (usually using SPSS). Get the p-value.
5 Interpret and conclude If p < 0.05 → Reject H₀ (significant result). If p ≥ 0.05 → Fail to reject H₀.
💡 What Does p-value Mean?

The p-value is the probability of seeing your results by random chance if H₀ were actually true.
p = 0.03 means: "There's only a 3% chance these results happened by luck." → Reject H₀ (it's unlikely to be coincidence).
p = 0.40 means: "There's a 40% chance these results happened by luck." → Do NOT reject H₀.

Hypothesis Testing

Type I & Type II Errors

Even when you do everything right, there's always a chance you could be wrong. Statistics gives names to the two types of mistakes you can make.

H₀ is actually TRUEH₀ is actually FALSE
You REJECT H₀ ❌ Type I Error (False Positive)
You said something is happening when it actually isn't		 ✅ Correct Decision
You correctly identified a real effect
You FAIL to reject H₀ ✅ Correct Decision
H₀ was true and you kept it		 ⚠️ Type II Error (False Negative)
You missed a real effect — said nothing's happening when it is

🚨 Type I Error (α)

  • False alarm — seeing an effect that isn't there
  • Controlled by setting α = 0.05
  • Example: Concluding a new drug works when it doesn't
  • More dangerous in medicine, less so in marketing

👻 Type II Error (β)

  • Missing a real effect — it exists but you didn't find it
  • Often happens with too-small sample sizes
  • Example: Concluding a drug has no effect when it actually helps
  • Reduce by increasing sample size
Hypothesis Testing

One-Tailed vs Two-Tailed Tests

Before running a test, you need to know if your prediction has a direction or not.

Test TypeH₁ FormWhen to UseExample
Two-Tailed H₁: μ₁ ≠ μ₂
(just "different")		 You predict a difference exists, but you don't know which group will be higher "Male and female managers score differently on communication (but unsure who scores higher)"
One-Tailed (Right) H₁: μ₁ > μ₂ You predict Group 1 will be higher/more than Group 2 "Male managers score higher than female managers on risk-taking"
One-Tailed (Left) H₁: μ₁ < μ₂ You predict Group 1 will be lower/less than Group 2 "Untrained staff score lower than trained staff on performance"
📌 Practical rule: If theory or previous studies give you a clear direction, use a one-tailed test. If you're unsure about direction, use two-tailed (it's more conservative and widely accepted in business research).
💡 Analogy

Imagine betting on a football match.
Two-tailed: "I bet the score won't be a draw" (either team could win).
One-tailed: "I bet Team A will win" (specific direction).

Normal Distribution

The most important concept in inferential statistics — and the foundation of most statistical tests.

Normal Distribution

The Bell Curve Explained

A normal distribution is when data naturally clusters around a central value and spreads out symmetrically in both directions — creating a bell shape. Most measurements in nature and business follow this pattern.

±1σ covers 68% of data ±2σ covers 95% of data ±3σ covers 99.7% of data
PropertyWhat It Means
SymmetricLeft half is a mirror image of the right half — no skew
Mean = Median = ModeAll three averages are the same value — all sit at the centre
Total area = 100%100% of all possible values fall somewhere under the curve
Never touches x-axisThe tails get very close to zero but theoretically never reach it
Normal Distribution

Testing for Normality

Before running most inferential tests, you need to check: is your data normally distributed? You can do this graphically or statistically.

📊

Histogram

Plot your data as a bar chart. If it looks like a bell — it's normal. If it leans left or right, it's skewed.

📦

Box & Whisker Plot

Shows median, quartiles and extremes. A symmetric box with equal whiskers suggests normality.

📉

Normal Q-Q Plot

Plots your data against a theoretical normal distribution. If points follow a straight 45° diagonal line → your data is normal.

TestUse WhenDecision Rule
Kolmogorov-Smirnov (K-S) Large samples (n ≥ 50) If p < 0.05 → NOT normal
If p ≥ 0.05 → Normal
Shapiro-Wilk Small samples (n < 50) If p < 0.05 → NOT normal
If p ≥ 0.05 → Normal
💡 Both tests use the same hypothesis:
H₀: Data is NOT significantly different from a normal distribution (i.e., it IS normal)
H₁: Data IS significantly different from normal (i.e., NOT normal)
A high p-value (≥ 0.05) is what you WANT — it means your data is normal.

Skewness and kurtosis are numbers that describe the shape of your distribution. SPSS reports these automatically.

Normal Distribution

What if Your Data is NOT Normal?

If your normality test fails (p < 0.05), you have two options:

🔄 Option 1: Transform the Data

  • Change the mathematical form to reduce the skew
  • Positive skew: try Log(x), 1/x, or √x
  • Negative skew: try squaring (x²) or cubing (x³)
  • Then re-test for normality

🔃 Option 2: Use Non-Parametric Tests

  • Non-parametric tests don't assume normality
  • Work with ordinal or categorical data too
  • Usually less powerful than parametric tests
  • Also preferred when n < 30

Paired t-testWilcoxon Signed Rank test

Parametric Tests

The t-Test Family

t-tests compare means when the population variance is unknown (which is almost always in business research). There are 3 types:

Question it answers: Does my sample's mean significantly differ from a known benchmark value?

📌 Example: A company claims its delivery takes an average of 3 days. You sample 40 deliveries and get a mean of 3.8 days. Is the true mean really 3 days or has it increased?
H₀: μ = 3 days | H₁: μ ≠ 3 days → Run one-sample t-test

Assumptions: Continuous scale (interval/ratio) · Normality · Random sample

Question it answers: Are the means of two different, unrelated groups significantly different?

📌 Example: Do male entrepreneurs and female entrepreneurs have different average monthly revenues? You survey 50 males and 50 females (different people). H₀: μ_male = μ_female | H₁: μ_male ≠ μ_female

Formula idea: t = (Mean₁ − Mean₂) / Standard Error of the difference

Assumptions: Continuous scale · Normality · Homogeneity of variance · Two separate categorical groups · Random sampling

Question it answers: Did the mean change significantly for the same group measured at two different times or under two conditions?

📌 Example: You measure the productivity of 30 employees before training and after training. Same 30 people, two scores. H₀: No difference between pre- and post-training scores | H₁: Post-training scores are higher

Also called: Repeated measures t-test, before-after test, husband-wife studies

Assumptions: Continuous scale · Normality · Related pairs · Homogeneity of variance

t-Test TypeGroupsKey QuestionDeveloped By
One-Sample1 group vs benchmark"Does my sample differ from a known value?"W.S. Gossett (1908) — published under pen name "Student" — hence "Student's t-test"
Independent2 different groups"Are these two groups different from each other?"
PairedSame group, 2 times/conditions"Did this group change between the two measurements?"
Parametric Tests

One-Way ANOVA

ANOVA = Analysis of Variance. When you want to compare means across more than two groups, a t-test isn't enough — you use ANOVA.

💡 Why Not Just Do Multiple t-Tests?

If you have 3 groups and run 3 separate t-tests, each at 5% error risk, your total error probability multiplies up. ANOVA tests all groups simultaneously in one test, keeping the error at 5%.

🎯

Purpose

Test if at least one group mean is significantly different from the others — with ONE test for ALL groups.

1️⃣

One-Way

One independent variable (called a "factor") with 3 or more levels/groups. E.g. Region: North, South, Central.

📊

How it works

Compares variance between groups to variance within groups. A large F-ratio → significant differences exist.

📌 Business Example

You want to know if employee performance differs across 4 departments: Finance, Marketing, HR, and Operations. You have 20 employees per department.
H₀: μ_Finance = μ_Marketing = μ_HR = μ_Operations (all means are equal)
H₁: At least one department mean is different
If ANOVA gives p < 0.05 → Reject H₀ → At least one department performs differently.

⚠️ Important: ANOVA tells you that differences exist — it does NOT tell you which groups are different. You need a Post-hoc test (like Tukey's HSD or Bonferroni) to find exactly which pairs differ.
AssumptionPlain English
Continuous scaleDV must be interval or ratio level
NormalityData in each group should be normally distributed
Homogeneity of VarianceThe spread (variance) in each group should be roughly equal — tested with Levene's test
IndependenceMore than 2 independent categorical groups
Random SamplingSubjects were randomly selected

Measuring the strength and direction of relationships between variables.

Correlation Analysis

Understanding Correlation

Correlation measures how strongly two variables are related and whether the relationship is positive or negative. It does NOT tell you that one causes the other.

Correlation Analysis

Pearson vs Spearman Correlation

There are two main correlation tests. Your choice depends on your data's scale and whether it's normally distributed.

📐 Pearson (r)

  • For interval or ratio (continuous) data
  • Data must be approximately normal
  • Relationship must be linear
  • Outliers should be minimal
  • Example: Correlation between salary (GHS) and years of experience

🏅 Spearman (ρ)

  • For ordinal or ranked data
  • No normality assumption needed
  • Use when data is not linear or has outliers
  • Also use when Pearson's assumptions are violated
  • Example: Correlation between satisfaction rank (1-5 scale) and loyalty rank
TypeAlso CalledUse ForKey Output
BivariateZero-order correlationSimple relationship between exactly 2 variablesr value and p-value
PartialControlled correlationRelationship between 2 variables while controlling for a thirdr value adjusted for control variable
💡 Partial Correlation Example

You find a correlation between age and salary. But older workers also tend to have more experience. Partial correlation lets you measure the age-salary relationship while holding experience constant — to see if age matters on its own.

Regression Analysis

From describing relationships to making actual predictions.

Regression Analysis

What is Regression?

Regression goes one step beyond correlation. Where correlation says "are these related?", regression says "if I know X, what will Y be?" — it lets you predict.

📈 Simple Linear Regression

  • One independent variable (X) predicts one dependent variable (Y)
  • Equation: Y = β₀ + β₁X + ε
  • β₀ = Y-intercept (value of Y when X = 0)
  • β₁ = slope (how much Y changes for each unit increase in X)
  • ε = error term (unexplained variation)

📊 Multiple Regression

  • Two or more independent variables predict Y
  • Equation: Y = β₀ + β₁X₁ + β₂X₂ + ... + βₖXₖ + ε
  • Controls for multiple factors simultaneously
  • More realistic for business (many factors affect outcomes)
  • Example: Sales predicted by advertising spend, price, and season
💡 Business Example

Simple: "For every additional GHS 1,000 spent on advertising (X), monthly revenue (Y) increases by GHS 4,500."
Equation: Revenue = 20,000 + 4.5 × Advertising Spend

Multiple: "Revenue is affected by advertising spend, number of sales staff, and time of year."
Revenue = 15,000 + 4.5(Advertising) + 8,000(Staff) + 2,000(Q4 dummy)

AssumptionPlain English
DV is continuousThe outcome variable must be on interval or ratio scale
LinearityThe relationship between X and Y should be a straight line (use scatter plot to check)
NormalityThe residuals (errors/differences between predicted and actual) should be normally distributed
HomoscedasticityThe spread of errors should be even/constant across all levels of X (no fan shape)
No multicollinearityIn multiple regression, IVs should not be highly correlated with each other
No autocorrelationErrors should be independent (important for time-series data)
Regression Analysis

Reading Regression Output (SPSS)

When you run a regression in SPSS, you get several key tables. Here's how to read them:

OutputWhat It ShowsWhat to Look For
R Correlation between predicted and actual Y values Closer to 1.0 = better fit
R² (R-squared) % of variation in Y explained by the model R² = 0.65 means your IVs explain 65% of why Y varies. Higher is better.
F-statistic (ANOVA table) Tests whether the overall model is significant If p < 0.05 → the model as a whole is significant
β (Beta coefficients) The slope — how much Y changes per unit of X Positive β = positive relationship; check p-value to see if each IV is significant
p-value (for each coefficient) Significance of each individual predictor If p < 0.05 → that variable significantly predicts Y; if p > 0.05 → it doesn't
💡 Sample Regression Interpretation

Output: R² = 0.72, F(2,97) = 124.3, p < 0.001
Training Hours: β = 0.52, p = 0.002 | Motivation: β = 0.38, p = 0.015

How to report it: "Training hours and motivation together explain 72% of the variance in employee performance (R² = .72, F = 124.3, p < .001). Training hours (β = .52, p = .002) and motivation (β = .38, p = .015) both significantly predict performance."
